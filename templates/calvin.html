Batting and Player Salary Analysis

(2013 - 2016)

We were looking to see if spending the big bucks could bring you the most hits. We broke the data into specific years (2013-2016) and took the total sum of hits for each team to compare. We then took the total salary that each team spent on players during those years and compared them with a stacked bar graph. After reviewing the data and the charts, we could not show any concrete results to show that the more money you spend the more hits you get. To see if there was any other connection we could make, we split each team into individual positions to show exactly where the money was going and what positions seem to produce the most runs. This data showed that each team was spending the most money on obtaining outfielders, and the least money on catchers. This trend was also seen in hits as the data shows that outfielders produce the most hits for each team, and catchers produce the least.

Wanted to see how the top 10 hitters stacked up against each other with strike outs and RBI’s. Split the data to take the top 10 hitters from 2013-2016 based on the number of actual hits. We then split the data into strike outs and RBI’s for each player to see how they compared. After looking at the data and the charts, there was no correlation we could make between their hits and RBI’s/Strike-outs. 10 Lowest hitting teams were Houston, Milwaukee, Philadelphia, Oakland, Cincinnati, Atlanta, Tampa Bay, Seattle, New York, San Diego.
